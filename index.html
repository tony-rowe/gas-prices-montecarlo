Regime-switching mechanics

Unlike a single OU process, this model uses three distinct regimes with time-varying transition probabilities. The war-premium regime (current) carries Brent at $100–120 with mean reversion toward the scenario target. The SPR-relief regime (Apr–Jun) adds a $10–15/bbl suppression effect from the IEA release, calibrated from Goldman's estimate that 254M bbl release offsets 50% of the supply shock. Post-normalization regime (Q3–Q4) is triggered stochastically — each day has a scenario-dependent probability of Hormuz partial re-opening, drawn from Dallas Fed quarterly model expectations.

Demand destruction ceiling

Oxford Economics: $140/bbl = global recession trigger, which itself suppresses demand and acts as a price ceiling through economic feedback. Wood Mackenzie: $150–200 "not outside the realms of possibility" but self-correcting via demand collapse. FHWA data: short-run elasticity −0.12 to −0.17 means a 30% price rise reduces consumption ~4%. Above $140 Brent, the model applies a graduated demand-destruction haircut that reduces the pump-price multiplier, reflecting that gasoline demand falls and refinery margins compress at extreme crude levels. This prevents the model from projecting $9+ prices that no analyst believes are stable end-states.

Oregon-specific factors

Oregon carries a persistent structural premium driven by: fuel tax $0.40/gal (AAA OR/ID); no in-state refineries; CARB-blend requirements; West Coast refinery utilisation at 79.6% (EIA, Feb 27 data); and the Olympic Pipeline outage in Feb that caused double-digit weekly spikes in OR and WA before BP restored operations Feb 19. Florence carries additional premium: coastal supply chain isolation, fewer than 8 retail stations, no pipeline access, and a summer tourist demand surge. Per KLCC: Portland gas was $5.39/gal on Mar 16. Florence typically runs $0.25–0.55/gal above Portland.

US supply offset (limited)

EIA: US crude production at 13.6–13.7M bbl/day (record), insulating US from some effects. Permian at 6.5M bbl/day. But Deutsche Bank, JPMorgan, and Goldman all agree: US shale cannot offset a 16M bbl/day gap. New Permian wells have 6–12 month lead times; 60% of Tier-1 acreage already drilled. The "Red Queen effect" means 83–85% of new drilling just offsets decline. US is a net energy exporter in aggregate but still prices gasoline against global Brent. The Trump administration ruled out export bans. Lifting Iranian sanctions (Treasury Sec Bessent suggestion) is politically untenable mid-conflict.

Bypass / relief factors

Saudi East-West Pipeline: 5M bbl/day capacity (temporarily expandable to 7M), currently being routed to Yanbu on the Red Sea. UAE pipeline: 1.5M bbl/day. Total bypass: 3.5–5.5M bbl/day (IEA, LSE estimate), leaving 14.5–16.5M bbl/day gap. IEA 400M bbl SPR release = 4 days of global consumption, or 20 days of Hormuz flows — a psychological backstop, not a structural fix. OPEC+ raised production 206,000 bbl/day in April — a rounding error vs. the disruption (Goldman). Pakistan's one tanker via Iran's channel for $2M is a data point on how desperate bypass is.

Why CI bands are wide — and should be

This situation is genuinely unprecedented. CRS (Congress.gov) notes: "a sustained 20M bbl/day supply disruption would create oil market conditions for which there is no historical precedent." This means:

  • No backtesting period exists to calibrate O-U parameters reliably
  • The Brent spike to $119 and drop to $80 on the same day (Mar 9→10) shows extreme bi-directional jump risk
  • Political resolution timelines are unknowable — Goldman changed its model from 10-day to 21-day within a week
  • Saudi Arabia's $180 modelling and Wood Mackenzie's $200 estimate are serious, not fringe
  • Demand destruction at $140 acts as a soft ceiling, but triggers a recession which itself reshapes the trajectory
The wide CI bands are the honest answer. Any model that shows a tight CI is overconfident.
